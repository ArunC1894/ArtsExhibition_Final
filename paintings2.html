Paintings of Jack B Yeats
The Liffey Swim
61 x 91cm

In this painting, Yeats tried to capture the thrilling atmosphere of an event that has been a part of Dublin annual sporting calendar since 1920. His portrayal of the event also signifies a return to the sporting subjects that had served as the inspiration for much of his earlier work. He asks his audience to participate in the event by placing them among those who push forward to catch a sight of the swimmer who surge towards the finishing line.

Morning after rain
758 × 1058 × 70 mm

Jack Yeats depicted the character and activities of the ordinary people of Western Ireland. He created a powerful, almost caricature-like sketch in outline, which he then directly painted in strong colours. Here is a man standing over the parapet of a bridge at Sligo into the muddy water of the river. His expression denotes a particular local character. Yeats knew the locals well because he grew up close to Sligo. In 1925, Yeats began painting in a very different style, using looser shapes, lighter colors, especially blues, and fictitious topics from Irish history.

For the road
61 x 92cm

Yeats showed a deep love of horses throughout his life. Horses are a recurring in his work. In this image, a ghostly horse gallops out of the darkness of a wood towards a bright, open space where a silhouetted figure waits. These horses represent freedom of movement, expressive energy, and good intentions.
